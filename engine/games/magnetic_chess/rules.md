# Magnetic Chess

Standard chess with one game-transforming rule: **every piece that moves acts as a magnet.** Invented by **João Pedro Neto** and **Claude Chaunier** at the end of 1996 (with corrections by Michael Keller). It is a Recognized Variant on the Chess Variant Pages. The authors call it *"a very dynamic variant, where things can turn against a player in a very fast way."*

## Objective
**Capture the enemy king.** There is *no check, no checkmate and no stalemate* — the player who captures the opponent's king wins immediately.

## Board & setup
The ordinary chess array on an 8×8 board: pawns on the second rank, and R N B Q K B N R behind them. White is player 1 (player 0 internally) and moves first; Black is player 2.

## Movement
All pieces move exactly as in FIDE chess (king, queen, rook, bishop, knight, pawn), **except**:

- **En passant is removed.**
- **Pawn double-step is relaxed.** A white pawn on the **1st or 2nd rank**, or a black pawn on the **7th or 8th rank**, may advance two squares — *even if it has moved before*. This matters because the magnet can push a pawn backward onto its home ranks, from where it can re-launch ("real missiles").
- **Castling** is entered as the king's two-square move (the rook follows automatically). Because there is no check, castling is **never** forbidden by attacks — the king may castle out of, through, or into an attacked square. The king and rook must simply be unmoved with the squares between them empty.

## The magnet
Every piece carries a magnetic charge — **White pieces one polarity, Black pieces the other** — **except the two kings, which are neutral.**

After a piece is moved to its destination square (and any capture on that square is resolved), it becomes a magnet. Look outward from that square along its **rank and file only — never the diagonals.** In each of the four orthogonal directions, find the **closest piece** and:

- **Repel** it if it has the **same charge** (same colour) — it slides directly *away* from the mover, continuing until it is stopped by another piece or the edge of the board (it comes to rest on the last empty square; it is never pushed off the board).
- **Attract** it if it has the **opposite charge** (other colour) — it slides *toward* the mover until it is **adjacent** to it.

Kings are special in three ways: a king is **never attracted or repelled**, **never attracts or repels** (so a plain king move produces no magnetic effect at all), and **blocks the field line** — nothing behind a king is affected. (In the rules-page diagram, the bishop on d8 is *not* attracted to a queen on d5 because the king on d6 stands between them.)

Only the four rays from the single moved piece fire, and they fire **once**: pieces displaced by the magnet do **not** themselves trigger further magnetism (no chain reaction). Pawns are pieces like any other — the effects apply to them too.

### Magnetic promotion
If the magnet drags a pawn onto its promotion rank (a white pawn to the 8th rank, a black pawn to the 1st), it **promotes to a Queen**. This is how a rook or bishop move can carry a `=Q` in the inventors' game scores.

### Worked example (from the rules page)
White queen d4 → d5. Before, along the queen's new rank and file: a friendly rook on c5, an enemy rook on f5, a white king on d6, an enemy bishop on d2 (and a bishop on d8 behind the king). After the move:

- **Rc5** (same charge) is repelled left, stopping on **b5** (blocked by the black queen on a5);
- **rf5** (opposite charge) is attracted in to **e5**, adjacent to the queen;
- **bd2** (opposite charge) is attracted up to **d4**, adjacent below the queen;
- **bd8** is untouched — the king on d6 blocks the file.

### Order of resolution
The four rays are independent — they share no square except the (occupied) square of the mover — so the displacements never collide and the order in which they are applied does not matter. This implementation computes all four from one snapshot and applies them together.

## Winning & draws
- **You win by capturing the enemy king.** Because kings are neutral and immovable by the magnet, a king can only ever leave the board by being captured.
- The game is drawn by the **fifty-move rule**, **threefold repetition**, and a hard ply cap (safety nets that also guarantee termination). There is **no insufficient-material draw** — even a lone king can win by capturing the other king.

## Implementation notes & rulings
The rules page is terse and leaves a few points implicit; the rulings below were chosen to match the stated mechanics and the inventors' sample games.

- **Magnetic promotion is forced to Queen.** The sample games only ever show `=Q` for a pawn moved by the field, and there is no natural way to offer a choice mid-move. A pawn *you* push to the last rank yourself promotes with the usual Q/R/B/N picker.
- **Castling's magnetic field is generated by the rook.** The page says "in castling, the changes are made by the rook movement." Since the king is neutral, the field after castling emanates from the **rook's** new square, treating the rook as the moving magnet.
- **No chain reactions.** A piece moved by the magnet does not fire its own field — the magnet resolves once per turn, from the moved piece only.
- **You may move into "check".** With no notion of check, any pseudo-legal move is legal — you may leave your king capturable (that is how you lose) and the two kings may even stand adjacent, one able to capture the other.
- **A player with no legal move at all** (extraordinarily rare, since a king is normally always capturable or mobile) is scored an honest **draw** — there is no stalemate rule, and no king was captured.
